\section{Results}
\label{sec:precise-alignment-results}
% ===========================================================================

This section reports quantitative results for both the LiDAR and stereo precise-alignment pipelines across seven experimental trials conducted in the indoor laboratory environment described in \cref{subsubsec:pa-test-environment}. The seven stereo trials (\texttt{stereo\_bag\_02}--\texttt{stereo\_bag\_08}) correspond directly by index to the seven LiDAR trials (\texttt{lidar\_job\_02}--\texttt{lidar\_job\_08}). The LiDAR pipeline generated 35 perturbation hypotheses per trial, yielding 245 hypothesis-level results in total; the stereo pipeline executes a single ICP run per trial and consequently produces one result per trial. LiDAR results are presented first: \cref{subsec:pa-results-registration-summary} summarises per-trial registration outcomes; \cref{subsec:pa-results-perturbation-groups} presents the per-perturbation-group breakdown; \cref{subsec:pa-results-transformation} reports the recovered transformation components; \cref{subsec:pa-results-convergence} analyses convergence consistency; \cref{subsec:pa-results-timing} documents pipeline timing; and \cref{subsec:pa-results-baseline-comparison} compares the graduated strategy against a Huber-only baseline. Stereo results follow in \cref{subsec:pa-results-stereo-summary}--\cref{subsec:pa-results-stereo-timing}, and \cref{subsec:pa-results-cross-modal} presents a cross-modal comparison.

\paragraph{Registration approach.}
The registration results reported here were produced by a graduated GICP strategy in which the Cauchy loss applied to LiDAR source clouds (described in \cref{subsec:pa-icp-registration}) is preceded by an initial alignment phase using a Huber loss. Specifically, the pipeline executes two sequential GICP phases for each hypothesis: Phase~1 applies a Huber loss ($k = 1.5$) for 30 iterations to achieve broad alignment, and Phase~2 applies a Cauchy loss ($k = 0.5$) for 20 iterations to refine the result. In addition, a rotation constraint clamps corrections to the pitch and roll axes to $\pm 5^{\circ}$ per iteration, whilst yaw and all three translation components remain unconstrained. A Huber-only baseline (Phase~1 only, 50 iterations, no rotation constraint) is included for comparison in \cref{subsec:pa-results-baseline-comparison}.

\paragraph{Evaluation context.}
As noted in \cref{subsubsec:pa-eval-metrics}, no external six-degree-of-freedom ground truth was available for these trials. All metrics --- fitness score and inlier RMSE --- are internal registration measures that quantify geometric consistency between the sensor observation and the 3DGS reference model, rather than absolute pose accuracy. A high fitness score and low RMSE are necessary but not sufficient for correct alignment; visual overlay (pending, see \cref{subsec:pa-results-visual-overlay}) provides a qualitative cross-check. Furthermore, the mapping between trial identifiers (\texttt{lidar\_job\_02}--\texttt{lidar\_job\_08} and \texttt{stereo\_bag\_02}--\texttt{stereo\_bag\_08}) and the trial conditions defined in \cref{tab:pa-trial-matrix} (D40/D60/D80 $\times$ A$-$15/A0/A$+$15) has not yet been established. Condition-specific analyses for both modalities --- including distance effects, approach-angle effects, and the replicate-pair repeatability check --- are therefore deferred to a future revision of this section.

\subsection{Per-Perturbation-Group Breakdown}
\label{subsec:pa-results-perturbation-groups}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The 35 hypotheses generated per trial are organised into five perturbation groups, described below and summarised in \cref{tab:pa-results-groups}. Statistics in the table are aggregated across all seven trials; group counts therefore reflect the combined 245 hypotheses.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Orig} (1 hypothesis per trial): the nominal, unperturbed initial pose.
  \item \emph{Group~A} (6 per trial): axis-aligned translation-only perturbations at half the nominal translation magnitude ($\delta_t / 2 = 5$\,cm) in the $\pm x$, $\pm y$, and $\pm z$ directions.
  \item \emph{Group~B} (4 per trial): cardinal-direction translations in the horizontal plane at the full nominal magnitude ($\delta_t = 10$\,cm), covering north, east, south, and west.
  \item \emph{Group~C} (12 per trial): compound perturbations combining diagonal horizontal translations ($\delta_t$) with yaw rotations ($\pm \delta_r$), sampling the four diagonal directions (NE, SE, SW, NW) in combination with positive and negative yaw perturbations.
  \item \emph{Group~E} (12 per trial): rotation-only perturbations sweeping roll, pitch, and yaw about each axis at both half-magnitude ($\delta_r / 2$) and full magnitude ($\delta_r$), with both positive and negative signs.
\end{itemize}

Acceptance rates are broadly similar across all five groups, ranging from 82\% (Group~C) to 86\% (Orig, A, and B). The slightly lower acceptance rate for Group~C is consistent with the compound nature of those perturbations, which combine a large horizontal displacement with a yaw rotation and therefore represent the most challenging initial poses for the registration solver. Despite this, the difference is small in absolute terms: three additional hypotheses per group would have to be accepted to equalise the rates.

Median fitness is consistent across groups (0.9107--0.9142), and the median inlier RMSE is identical at 33.25\,mm for Groups~A, B, C, and E, with the Orig group at 34.64\,mm. The slight elevation in the Orig group RMSE reflects the contribution of \texttt{lidar\_job\_03}, which has the highest per-trial median RMSE at 34.93\,mm. These results suggest that the registration outcome is not sensitive to the class of initial perturbation applied, provided the hypothesis is accepted.

\texttt{lidar\_job\_05} achieves near-zero spread across all components (all $\sigma < 0.1$\,mm and $< 0.01^{\circ}$), indicating that all 32 accepted hypotheses converge to a single pose to within numerical precision. \texttt{lidar\_job\_04} and \texttt{lidar\_job\_08} show elevated translational spread ($\sigma_{t_x} > 0.07$\,m), whilst \texttt{lidar\_job\_07} and \texttt{lidar\_job\_08} exhibit the largest rotational spread in roll ($\sigma_\phi > 4^{\circ}$). \subsubsection*{Inter-Trial Consistency}

Excluding the non-converging \texttt{lidar\_job\_02}, 6 of 7 trials produced at least 32 accepted hypotheses. Across these six converging trials, the best-hypothesis fitness scores range from 0.9018 (\texttt{lidar\_job\_07}) to 0.9277 (\texttt{lidar\_job\_05}), with a mean of 0.9161 and standard deviation of 0.0086. The corresponding best-hypothesis inlier RMSE values range from 23.41\,mm (\texttt{lidar\_job\_08}) to 36.27\,mm (\texttt{lidar\_job\_03}), with a mean of 31.19\,mm and standard deviation of 5.23\,mm. The consistency of the fitness score across trials (coefficient of variation $< 1\%$) indicates that the pipeline reliably achieves a similar quality of geometric correspondence regardless of the initial sensor pose, for the range of conditions tested. The larger relative variation in RMSE (coefficient of variation $\approx 17\%$) likely reflects condition-specific factors such as viewing angle and sensor-to-target distance.

\subsubsection*{Cross-Job Trend: Perturbation Benefit}

For most converging trials, the original (unperturbed) hypothesis achieves the same fitness and RMSE as the best-selected hypothesis across all 35 perturbations, with the fitness improvement due to perturbation ranging from 0.0000 to 0.0042. In no trial does neighbourhood perturbation produce a reduction in RMSE relative to the unperturbed result. This indicates that, for the conditions evaluated, the initial poses constructed from manual measurement (\cref{subsubsec:pa-initial-pose}) were already within the convergence basin of the global optimum; the perturbation strategy provides robustness against larger initial pose errors without degrading the result when the initial estimate is accurate. Quantitative analysis of the perturbation benefit as a function of initial pose error magnitude requires the replicate-pair consistency check and cross-condition trend analysis, both of which are deferred pending condition-label mapping.

\subsubsection*{Replicate-Pair Consistency}

The trial design includes one replicate of the centre condition (D60A0 and D60A0r, see \cref{tab:pa-trial-matrix}) as a repeatability check. This analysis is deferred because the mapping between job identifiers and trial condition labels has not yet been established.

Across all seven trials, total pipeline latency ranges from 8.06\,s (\texttt{stereo\_bag\_02}, which has an anomalously small source cloud) to 15.83\,s (\texttt{stereo\_bag\_05}), with a mean of $11.90 \pm 2.39$\,s. The stereo pipeline does not require the 25\,s scan accumulation interval that precedes the LiDAR pipeline, and evaluates only a single ICP hypothesis per trial rather than 35. The total stereo latency of approximately 12\,s is therefore substantially lower than the LiDAR total pipeline latency of approximately 48\,s. This latency advantage comes at the cost of reduced robustness: a single-hypothesis strategy has no fallback if the initial pose estimate lies outside the ICP convergence basin, whereas the LiDAR perturbation strategy provides coverage across a range of initial pose errors.

Several observations emerge from the matched comparison.

\paragraph{Convergence reliability.} LiDAR achieved full or near-full hypothesis acceptance in six of seven trials (86\%), whereas the stereo pipeline converged in only four of seven (57\%). Trial~02 failed for both modalities --- LiDAR due to a ground segmentation failure (Patchwork++) and stereo due to an analogous trunk segmentation collapse --- suggesting a common scene-level difficulty in that trial. Trials~06 and 08 converged for LiDAR but failed for stereo; in both cases the LiDAR pipeline's 35-hypothesis perturbation strategy provided recovery from initial pose errors that exceeded the convergence basin of the single-hypothesis stereo ICP. No trial that failed for LiDAR succeeded for stereo.

\paragraph{Registration quality.} On the four trials where both pipelines converged (03, 04, 05, 07), the stereo inlier RMSE is consistently and substantially lower than the LiDAR median RMSE: 15.81\,mm versus 31.22\,mm on average, a reduction of approximately 49\%. This difference reflects several factors that are difficult to fully disentangle. The stereo source cloud, clipped at $z_\mathrm{far} = 1.0$\,m, captures only the near-field portion of the vine surface, within which FoundationStereo depth estimation achieves high spatial accuracy. The LiDAR source cloud, accumulated over 25\,s across a wider angular extent, captures a larger fraction of the vine structure but incorporates returns from surface orientations and distances that carry higher geometric uncertainty. Since the inlier RMSE quantifies geometric consistency only within the region of matched correspondences, the spatially restricted stereo cloud may produce a systematically lower RMSE without implying superior global pose accuracy.

\paragraph{Pipeline latency.} The stereo pipeline completes in approximately 12\,s per trial (mean $11.90 \pm 2.39$\,s) with no preceding accumulation phase, compared to approximately 48\,s total latency for the LiDAR pipeline. The stereo pipeline is therefore markedly faster per alignment event, though the LiDAR pipeline provides substantially greater robustness through its multi-hypothesis perturbation strategy.

\subsection{Scope and Pending Analyses}
\label{subsec:pa-results-scope}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The results presented in this section are limited in two respects, which constrain the conclusions that can be drawn at this stage.

\textbf{Condition-label mapping.} The trial identifiers used throughout this section (\texttt{lidar\_job\_02}--\texttt{lidar\_job\_08} and \texttt{stereo\_bag\_02}--\texttt{stereo\_bag\_08}) have not yet been mapped to the trial conditions defined in \cref{tab:pa-trial-matrix}. Consequently, condition-specific analyses for both modalities --- distance effects, approach-angle effects, the replicate-pair repeatability check (D60A0 vs.\ D60A0r), and cross-modal per-condition comparison --- cannot yet be reported. These analyses will be incorporated once the mapping is established.

\textbf{Visual overlay.} Qualitative registration quality assessment via visual overlay (\cref{subsec:pa-results-visual-overlay}) is pending for both modalities and will be added in a future revision.
